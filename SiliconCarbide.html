01 / Overview

What is SiC?

A compound where silicon and carbon atoms bond in a very strong three-dimensional covalent network.

Compound Silicon Carbide
Formula SiC
Elements Silicon + Carbon
Type Covalent network solid
02 / Periodic Trends

Silicon vs. Carbon.

Atomic Radius

Si > C

Silicon has more electron shells. Down a group, atoms get larger.

Ionization Energy

C > Si

Carbon holds electrons tighter. Smaller atoms have stronger nuclear pull.

Electronegativity

C > Si

Carbon ≈ 2.55, Silicon ≈ 1.90. Electronegativity decreases down a group.

Electron Affinity

Complex

Electron affinity is harder to compare directly because this trend is less simple than atomic radius or electronegativity.

03 / Structure

A 3D covalent network.

Every silicon atom bonds to four carbons. Every carbon bonds to four silicons. The result is a rigid lattice extending in all directions.

Strong directional covalent bonds throughout

Tetrahedral geometry at every atom

One giant structure — no individual molecules

06 / Applications

Where SiC matters.

SiC is used across several industries where high temperature and high voltage performance are needed.

EV Power Systems

Converts DC battery power to AC motor drive with far less energy loss.

Solar Inverters

Handles high voltage from solar arrays with higher conversion efficiency.

Industrial Power

Railways, grids, and factories rely on SiC for stable high-power switching.

Abrasives

Mohs hardness ~9. Ideal for grinding, cutting, and polishing hard materials.

10 / Impact

From atoms to energy systems.

SiC's atomic structure gives it electronic properties that matter in real industrial systems.

Electric vehicles

SiC devices reduce energy loss during power conversion, which helps improve the efficiency of EV drivetrains.

Solar power

SiC inverters handle high voltage from solar panels with less energy loss than traditional silicon inverters.

Industrial power

SiC components operate reliably under high temperature and high voltage stress in grid and railway systems.

Why it matters

Because of its atomic structure, SiC converts and controls electrical power more efficiently than many alternatives.

Conclusion

Structure drives function.

SiC matters because its atomic structure — a rigid 3D covalent network — gives it the hardness, heat resistance, and electronic properties needed for modern power electronics.
